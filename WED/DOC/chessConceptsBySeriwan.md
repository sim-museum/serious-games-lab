Chess Basics:

*   Chess board has 8 ranks and 8 files (a-h). Each square has a corresponding letter and number.
*   White pieces start on ranks 1-2, black pieces on ranks 7-8.
*   White square always in bottom right corner when setting up board.
*   Three types of positions based on number of pawns: closed (all 16 pawns), half-open (14-15 pawns), open (13 or fewer pawns)

Piece Values & Characteristics:

*   Queen worth 9 points. Most powerful and mobile piece. Works best in open positions.
*   Rook worth 5 points. Also works best in open positions, especially strong in endgames. Good defenders.
*   Bishop worth 3 points. Works best in open positions. Can be clumsy in openings.
*   Knight worth 3 points. Unlike other pieces, works best in half-open and closed positions.
*   King worth 3.5 points to masters for attacking purposes. Most important piece - if checkmated, game is over.
*   Pawns worth 1 point. Least mobile and valuable piece.

The Four Elements of Chess:

1.  Force (material) - most important element. Being up in force count means you are winning.
2.  Space - person who controls more space will have more active pieces and be the attacker.
3.  Time - person who develops pieces more quickly will control more space and be the attacker.
4.  Pawn structure - tells you where to place pieces. Determines open files, diagonals, king safety. Covered extensively later.

Seven Opening Principles:

1.  Control the center, especially the "sweet center" (d4, d5, e4, e5)
2.  Open with e-pawn or d-pawn
3.  Don't develop queen too early or she'll be attacked
4.  Develop knights before bishops
5.  Don't move the same piece twice in the opening
6.  Castle early to develop rook and shelter the king
7.  Don't attack without justification - must have an advantage in one of the four elements

Italian Game Opening:

1.  e4 e5 2. Nf3 Nc6 3. Bc4 Bc5 4. c3 Nf6 5. d4 (opens position) exd4 6. cxd4 Bb4+
2.  Bd2 (main line, avoids pin) Bxd2+ 8. Nbxd2 d5!? (black counterattacks)
3.  exd5 Nxd5 10. Qb3 (sets up pin on knight) Nce7 11. O-O (castles kingside) O-O

*   After 11...O-O, opening is over. Evaluate with the elements. White is up 7 to 4 in time (piece development), controls more space. Black's compensation is in the pawn structure (to be covered later).

Tactics Introduced:

*   Pin: Absolute pin (pinned piece can't legally move), relative pin (pinned piece can move but will lose the piece behind it)
*   Double attack: Two pieces attacked simultaneously
*   Battery: Two pieces lined up on same rank, file or diagonal (e.g. queen behind bishop)
*   Removal of the guard: Forcing a defensive piece to move so it no longer guards another piece
*   Zwischenzug (in-between move): Responding to a threat with a threat of your own, forcing the opponent to deal with your threat first
*   Underpromotion: Promoting a pawn to a piece other than a queen to avoid stalemate

Pawn Structures:  
Three categories - weak, balanced, dynamic  
Weak Pawn Structures (most important to understand):

1.  Pawn islands: Groups of pawns separated by open files. The more pawn islands, the weaker. Require piece protection.
2.  Isolated pawn: Can't be protected by another pawn. Square in front is a weakness. Especially weak when isolated pawn is a central d-pawn (called an isolani).
3.  Doubled pawns: Two pawns of same color on same file. Less mobile, become targets. Push the front one if you have them. Avoid making them if you can.
4.  Backward pawn: Can't advance without being captured. Strategy is to restrain, blockade and destroy it. Creates a cramped game, bad bishop.
5.  Hanging pawns: Two pawns side-by-side with no pawns to support them. Can become weak if opponent forces them to advance.

Balanced Pawn Structures:

1.  Symmetrical pawn chains (e.g. White c3, d4, e3 vs Black c6, d5, e6) - quiet, simplified positions. Pawns rarely do the work.
2.  Locked center - play revolves around pawn breaks on the wings. Goal is to attack the base of opponent's pawn chain.

Dynamic Pawn Structures:

1.  Passed pawn: Can advance to queening square without being stopped by enemy pawns. Worth more in endgame with fewer pieces on board. Must be blockaded and destroyed. Protected and connected passed pawns are even stronger.
2.  Pawn majority: Having more pawns than opponent on one side of the board. Goal is to create a passed pawn.
3.  Minority attack: Advancing your fewer pawns on one side to create a weak pawn for opponent, usually backward pawn on an open file.

Other Concepts:

*   Outposts: Squares that can't be attacked by enemy pawns, especially good for knights. White likes e5 and d6, Black likes e4 and d3.
*   Good vs bad bishops: Avoid putting pawns on same color as your bishop. Enemy pawns on your bishop's color are targets.
*   Fianchetto: Developing bishop to b2/g2 or b7/g7. Influences long diagonal, supports castled king.
*   Prophylaxis: Preventing opponent's threats and plans before they happen.
*   Overprotection: Defending a key point with more pieces than seem necessary.
*   Do not exchange attacking pieces for defending pieces if you have the attack.
*   Rooks belong behind passed pawns, on open files, and on the 7th rank.
*   In the endgame, the king becomes a strong piece. Centralize it and use it actively.
*   Visualize the position 3-4 moves ahead, especially with dynamic pawn structures. Practice this skill with tactical puzzles.

Elementary Checkmates:

*   Fool's Mate: 1. f3 e5 2. g4 Qh4# (fastest possible mate)
*   Scholar's Mate: 1. e4 e5 2. Qh5 Nc6 3. Bc4 Nf6?? 4. Qxf7#
*   Légal's Mate: 1. e4 e5 2. Nf3 d6 3. Bc4 Bg4 4. Nc3 g6?? 5. Nxe5! Bxd1?? 6. Bxf7+ Ke7 7. Nd5#
